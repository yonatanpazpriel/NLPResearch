We the undersigned support Sikhism's inclusion in a more meaningful way in California's Ethnic Studies Model Curriculum. Sikhism is the world's fifth largest religion with more than 25 million followers around the globe, yet it is often excluded from instruction in American public and private schools; the Ethnic Studies Curriculum presents an opportunity to ensure its appropriate presence in classroom instruction.

Roughly half of the 500,000 Sikhs in the United States are estimated to live in California, and Sikhs have been an integral part of this state's social fabric since they first arrived in the country more than 125 years ago. In fact, the first Sikh gurdwara, or house of worship, in the United States was founded in Stockton, California, in 1912 and still welcomes worshippers and visitors today. Moreover, many notable Sikh Americans have ties to California. Dalip Singh Saund, the first Asian American to serve in Congress-who also identified as Sikh--served California's 29th congressional district from 1957 to 1963; Sikh physicist Narinder Singh Kapany, who is widely considered to be "the Father of Fiber Optics," has taught and researched at several California universities, including the University of California Berkeley, the University of California Santa Cruz, and Stanford University.

Sikh Americans help make up the backbone of California as farmers, drivers, engineers, and construction workers. They appear in all walks of life, from teachers and pilots to musicians and doctors, and from civil servants and social activists to philanthropists and movie actors.

Ensuring that Sikh culture and history is present in California's Ethnic Studies Model Curriculum will not only make for a more comprehensive education for all of our state's students, but safer classrooms for Sikh children: visibility and familiarity are the first steps to combating bullying. And while it is true that Sikh children experience a rate of bullying estimated to be twice that of the national average--and that Sikhs in general are the third-most targeted religious group in hate crimes across the country, per the FBI--we refuse to let victimhood or marginalization define the rich, diverse Sikh American experience. This positive assertion of our identity begins with the proactive inclusion of the contributions of Sikhs to our state and our nation through the Ethnic Studies Curriculum.

Two community-based Sikh organizations with large, overlapping constituencies in California, the Sikh Coalition and the Jakara Movement, have submitted a detailed request for revisions and inclusions to the present draft Ethnic Studies model curriculum, and a draft lesson plan for proposed insertion. We urge you to consider and adopt their proposal so that Sikh families in our state will see themselves reflected and represented in the Ethnic Studies Curriculum and in their classrooms. Thank you for your consideration, and for your pursuit of an education system that embraces the diversity and pluralism that makes our nation strong.
